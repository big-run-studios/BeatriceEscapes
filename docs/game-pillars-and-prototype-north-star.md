# Bea Escapes — Game Pillars & Prototype North Star

> **One-Line Vision**
> A family on the run discovers they're more magical — and more powerful together — than they ever knew, in a roguelite beat 'em up that plays like the best modern arcade brawler and feels like your favorite animated movie.

---

## 1. Game Pillars

### Pillar 1 — Family-First Action

Every mechanic reinforces the Bell family bond. Characters are mechanically asymmetric but emotionally united. The escape only works because they're together.

- Each character's playstyle reflects their personality and family role.
- Co-op interactions (revives, synergy combos, positional teamwork) reward playing as a unit.
- Bea is the emotional and mechanical center — her magic is why they run, and why they can try again.
- Progression deepens family identity: unlocking new abilities makes each member feel more like *themselves*, not generic power growth.

### Pillar 2 — Best-in-Class Beat 'Em Up Combat

Combat must feel as satisfying as the genre's best: punchy, readable, expressive, and immediately gratifying with hidden depth underneath.

- Strong hit-feel through hitstop, screenshake, knockback arcs, and impactful SFX.
- Layered combo system: simple chains feel great on first play; directional variants, launchers, juggles, and cancels reward mastery.
- Per-character defensive identity (block, parry, dodge, weave) gives each family member a distinct combat rhythm.
- Enemy telegraphs are generous and clear. Readability is never sacrificed for spectacle.

### Pillar 3 — Roguelite Momentum

Every run builds toward something. Stacking boons create escalating power fantasies. Meta-unlocks expand the possibility space without inflating raw stats.

- Runs are 25–35 minutes: long enough to build a satisfying loadout, short enough to immediately start another.
- Stacking magical boons with synergies create unique builds per run (Hades/Vampire Survivors model).
- Meta-progression unlocks *options*, not power — new boon pools, wizard contacts, event types, route possibilities, and story revelations.
- The Hades-style story loop means successful escapes advance the narrative toward a true ending, so winning never feels like the end.

### Pillar 4 — Surprising Escape Adventure

Procedural structure must feel authored, not random. Every run should feel like a unique escape story worth retelling.

- Hades-style room-to-room navigation with reward-preview doors keeps momentum constant.
- Handcrafted encounter chunks assembled procedurally within authored biome constraints.
- Events, wizard encounters, shops, and narrative beats punctuate combat with variety.
- Route choices create meaningful risk/reward decisions without ever stopping the forward momentum.

### Pillar 5 — Broad Appeal, Deep Mastery

The game is E10+, playable by families, and deep enough to obsess enthusiast players. When choosing between complexity and clarity, choose clarity. When choosing between realism and fun, choose fun.

- Simple inputs produce satisfying results immediately; depth is discovered, never required.
- Tone stays adventurous and heartfelt — tense without becoming grim, funny without becoming ironic.
- Content avoids graphic violence, cruelty, hopelessness, and age-inappropriate threat.
- 4-player co-op naturally accommodates mixed skill levels since characters range from accessible (Luna's instinctive play) to technical (Heather's parry timing).

## 2. Tone & Creative North Star

### Reference Touchstones

| | |
|---|---|
| **Story/Tone** | *The Mitchells vs. the Machines* — family chaos, sincerity, humor under pressure |
| **Visual Ambition** | *Hades* — painterly, dimensional, premium 2D with depth |
| **Combat Feel** | *TMNT: Shredder's Revenge* / *Scott Pilgrim* — arcade clarity, satisfying combos |
| **Structure** | *Hades* — room-to-room, reward doors, stacking boons, narrative loop |
| **Replayability** | *Hades* + *Vampire Survivors* — build variety, escalating power fantasy |

### What the Tone IS

- Serious but funny
- Emotionally sincere — the family bond is real and earnable
- Adventurous, chaotic, and charming
- Tense without becoming grim
- Cinematic but playful
- Stakes are real; danger is exciting, not traumatizing

### What the Tone IS NOT

- Grimdark, dystopian, or bleak
- Ironic or detached
- Politically literal (MaRC is threatening and authoritarian, not a real-world allegory)
- Cruel or punishing in its storytelling
- Horror-adjacent in any way

### Where Humor Lives

- Family personality clashes under pressure
- Improvised escape solutions gone sideways
- Absurd magical side effects (Bea sneezes and a mailbox becomes a frog)
- Luna doing dog things at the worst possible moment
- Neighborhood wizard weirdness
- MaRC grunt incompetence and escalating bureaucratic chaos
- John's inventions working in ways he didn't intend

## 4. Run Structure

### The Arc of an Escape

Each run is one attempt to flee town through 4 biome areas. Target duration: **25–35 minutes** for a successful run.

```
NEIGHBORHOOD ──→ TOWN CENTER ──→ INDUSTRIAL EDGE ──→ OUTER WILD ──→ ESCAPE
   (Panic)        (Stabilize)      (Escalate)         (Climax)      (Resolution)
```

### Pacing Model

| Phase | Biome | Feeling | Design Intent |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Panic** | Neighborhood | Scramble, improvise, survive | Teach mechanics under gentle pressure. First boons arrive. |
| **Stabilize** | Town Center | Find footing, start building | Build identity emerges. Route choices matter. Wizard community appears. |
| **Escalate** | Industrial Edge | Pressure mounts, loadout tested | MaRC escalates. Elite enemies. Builds either pay off or struggle. |
| **Climax** | Outer Wild | All-out escape push | Strongest enemies, strongest boons, dramatic final encounters. |

### Room-to-Room Flow (Hades Model)

Within each biome area, the player moves through **5–7 rooms** selected from authored pools:

1. **Clear the room** — defeat enemies or complete an encounter objective.
2. **Choose a door** — 2–3 exits appear, each previewing its reward type:
   - Combat encounter (boon reward)
   - Elite encounter (rare boon reward)
   - Shop / wizard vendor
   - Wizard boon blessing
   - Story/event scene
   - Miniboss (area exit)
3. **Move forward** — enter the chosen room, repeat.

Each biome ends with a **miniboss** that gates progression to the next area. The final biome ends with the **escape boss**.

### Encounter Types

- **Standard combat** — 2–3 waves of MaRC enemies. Reward: common boon or resource.
- **Elite combat** — Tougher enemies with elite modifiers. Reward: rare boon.
- **Ambush** — Surprise attack with no reward preview. Reward: bonus resources.
- **Shop** — Spend collected resources on boons, healing, or utility items.
- **Wizard blessing** — Choose 1 of 3 offered magical boons (free).
- **Rescue event** — Help a wizard NPC in distress. Reward: unique boon or ally assist.
- **Family moment** — Brief narrative beat (banter, encouragement, argument). Reward: small stat buff or morale bonus.
- **Miniboss** — Scripted boss fight gating the next biome.
- **Escape boss** — Final run boss. Defeat to complete the escape.

## 5. World & Biomes

### Area 1 — The Neighborhood

> *Birthday-party aftermath. Smoke from the fireball. Confused neighbors. Sirens in the distance.*

- **Setting:** Suburban streets, cul-de-sacs, backyards, playgrounds, driveways, alleys between houses.
- **Enemy Presence:** Light. First MaRC responders — basic agents, a patrol drone or two. Confused neighbors act as obstacles, not enemies.
- **Tone:** Scrambling, chaotic, almost comedic. The family is literally running from a birthday party.
- **Unique Elements:** Environmental objects from the party (folding tables as cover, balloon animals as distractions), shortcut through a neighbor's garage, hiding behind hedges.
- **Miniboss:** MaRC First Responder Squad Leader — overconfident field commander with a capture net launcher and a megaphone that summons backup.

### Area 2 — Town Center

> *The alert has spread. Shops are closing. MaRC checkpoints are going up. But help is hiding in plain sight.*

- **Setting:** Main street, intersections, storefronts, a park, a parking garage, a farmers market, public transit stops.
- **Enemy Presence:** Medium. MaRC has deployed organized squads, shield units, and early anti-magic tech. Checkpoints create bottleneck encounters.
- **Tone:** Tension rises but the wizard community starts revealing itself — the bookshop owner flashes a hidden sigil, a food cart vendor slips the family a charm.
- **Unique Elements:** Civilian crowds (MaRC won't attack recklessly in public, creating tactical opportunities), wizard safehouses disguised as mundane shops, environmental verticality (rooftops, fire escapes).
- **Miniboss:** MaRC Checkpoint Commander — deploys suppression barriers, directs shield-wall formations, calls in drone surveillance.

### Area 3 — Industrial Edge

> *The public zone is behind them. MaRC has dropped the pretense. This is a pursuit now.*

- **Setting:** Warehouses, rail yards, bus depots, service roads, utility corridors, construction sites, an overpass.
- **Enemy Presence:** Heavy. Elite MaRC pursuers, anti-magic technicians, tracking drones hunting actively. Gadget specialists deploying traps.
- **Tone:** Urgent, high-pressure. The family is being hunted. Encounters are more aggressive, less forgiving. But the family is also more powerful now — their builds should be coming online.
- **Unique Elements:** Moving train cars as dynamic terrain, warehouse traps, electrical hazards, forklift jousting (because fun > realism).
- **Miniboss:** MaRC Pursuit Specialist — fast, relentless hunter with anti-magic gear, tracking abilities, and a capture mech suit.

### Area 4 — The Outer Wild

> *Past the edge of town. Old roads, dense woods, abandoned places. The wizard community is strongest here — and so is MaRC's final line.*

- **Setting:** Forest paths, overgrown roads, an abandoned gas station, a hidden wizard waystation, ancient magical landmarks, the threshold of escape.
- **Enemy Presence:** Maximum. MaRC's best agents and heaviest tech. But also the strongest wizard allies and the most powerful boons.
- **Tone:** Epic and emotional. The family is at peak power. The magic is real, it's theirs, and they're almost free. The final push should feel triumphant and desperate in equal measure.
- **Unique Elements:** Magical terrain (glowing ley lines that buff abilities, ancient wards that block MaRC tech), wizard safe-houses with powerful blessings, environmental magic (trees that move, paths that shift).
- **Escape Boss:** MaRC Regional Director — arrives in a command vehicle with heavy weaponry, anti-magic field generators, and elite guard squad. A multi-phase fight that tests the family's full build.

## 6. Combat Design

### Core Loop

```
APPROACH ──→ ENGAGE ──→ COMBO ──→ SPECIAL ──→ REPOSITION
    ↑                                              │
    └──────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Every character follows this loop but expresses it differently:
- **Andrew:** Slow approach, absorb hits on engage, short devastating combos, room-clearing specials, hold position.
- **Heather:** Measured approach, parry on engage, precise combo chains, amplify-pulse specials, reposition to buff allies.
- **John:** Fast approach, dash through engage, quick hit-and-run combos, gadget specials, reposition constantly.
- **Luna:** Instant approach, weave through engage, sustained bite combos, takedown specials, never stop moving.

### Layered Combo System

**Surface layer (accessible):**
- Light attack chain (3–4 hits) → auto-finisher
- Heavy attack (slow, high damage, knockback)
- Special ability (character-specific, cooldown or resource gated)
- Defense action (block / parry / dodge / weave)

**Depth layer (discoverable):**
- Directional combo variants (forward+light = lunging strike, down+light = sweep)
- Launchers (up+heavy sends enemies airborne)
- Juggle chains (light attacks connect on airborne enemies)
- Slam finishers (down+heavy on airborne enemy = spike to ground, area damage)
- Cancel windows (dodge-cancel out of combo recovery for safety or repositioning)
- Dash attacks (attack out of dodge for gap-closing openers)

**Mastery layer (rewarding):**
- Parry counters into combo starters (Heather)
- Block-cancel into grab (Andrew)
- Gadget-cancel chains (John: attack → gadget → attack without dropping combo)
- Momentum-enhanced finishers (Luna: high momentum changes her finisher animations and damage)

### Hit-Feel Standards

Every attack must satisfy these minimums:
- **Hitstop:** 2–4 frames on contact (scaled by attack weight)
- **Screenshake:** Subtle on light hits, pronounced on heavies and specials
- **Knockback:** Enemies visibly react — stagger, slide, launch, or crumple depending on attack type
- **SFX:** Distinct impact sounds per attack weight; magical attacks get layered elemental SFX
- **VFX:** Hit sparks, magic trails, and impact flashes scaled to attack significance
- **Camera:** Subtle zoom on heavy hits; brief slow-motion on kills and special finishers

### Defensive Identity

| Character | Primary Defense | Feel | Risk/Reward |
|---|---|---|---|
| Andrew | Block | Hold to absorb damage (reduced). Costs stamina. | Low risk, stamina management. Can block for allies near him. |
| Heather | Parry | Timed press to deflect and counter. Tight window. | High risk, high reward. Perfect parry triggers Catalyst Pulse (buff + stagger). |
| John | Dodge | Dash with i-frames. Covers distance. | Medium risk. Repositions aggressively. Dash-through deals chip damage. |
| Luna | Weave | Passive auto-dodge chance that scales with momentum. | Rewards aggression. Low momentum = vulnerable. High momentum = nearly untouchable. |

### Revive System

- **Default:** Timed proximity revive. Stand near a downed ally for ~3 seconds to revive them.
- **Combat Revive (boon unlock):** Hitting enemies near a downed ally fills a revive meter. Rewards aggression over passivity.
- **Bea Revive (boon unlock):** Bea channels a burst of magic to instantly revive. Limited charges per run. Flashy and emotionally resonant.
- **Catalyst Revive (Heather-specific boon):** Heather's perfect parry near a downed ally instantly revives them. High-skill, high-drama option.

### Co-op Synergy Interactions

- **Combo Chain:** When two characters hit the same enemy in quick succession, a synergy bonus triggers (extra damage, extended hitstun, or a magic burst).
- **Launch Assist:** One character launches an enemy; another character's air attack on that enemy deals bonus damage and triggers a team finisher animation.
- **Positional Amplify:** Heather's proximity aura stacks with ally actions — Andrew's block absorbs more, John's gadgets gain charges, Luna's momentum builds faster.
- **Protect:** Andrew can step in front of an attack aimed at an ally to absorb it with his block. Costs extra stamina but prevents ally damage entirely.

## 7. Progression

### In-Run Progression: Magical Boons

Boons are the primary build-shaping mechanic within a single run. They stack, synergize, and create emergent power combinations.

**Boon Sources:**
- Wizard blessings (choose 1 of 3 after certain rooms)
- Combat rewards (guaranteed after elite encounters)
- Shops (purchasable with run currency)
- Rescue events (unique boons from grateful wizard NPCs)
- Story beats (occasional boons tied to family moments)

**Boon Categories:**

- **Offensive** — Bonus damage types, combo extenders, attack speed, area effects.
  - *Example: "Spark Knuckles" — light attacks chain lightning to a nearby enemy.*
- **Defensive** — Shield pulses, damage reduction, healing on parry/dodge, revival upgrades.
  - *Example: "Stubborn Stance" — Andrew's block generates a shockwave when broken.*
- **Utility** — Movement speed, cooldown reduction, resource generation, room-clear bonuses.
  - *Example: "Quick Paws" — Luna gains a burst of speed after each takedown.*
- **Synergy** — Boons that enhance when combined with other specific boons or character proximity.
  - *Example: "Family Resonance" — each family member in the room increases all boon effects by 10%.*
- **Signature** — Character-specific boons that enhance or transform their core mechanics.
  - *Example: "Overcharged Shoulder Cast" — Bea's ranged attacks from Andrew's shoulders pierce through enemies.*

**Rarity Tiers:** Common → Uncommon → Rare → Legendary. Higher rarity from harder encounters or lucky shop finds. Legendary boons transform playstyle (e.g., "Luna's Howl — takedowns now fear all enemies on screen for 2 seconds").

**Stacking Rules:** Most boons stack with everything. Some legendary boons are mutually exclusive to force interesting choices. The goal is "every run feels like a different build" without requiring a PhD in synergy charts.

### Meta-Progression: The Wizard Network

Between runs, the family returns to a growing **wizard safehouse** that serves as the hub. Progression here unlocks *options*, not raw power.

**Unlock Categories:**

- **New Boon Pools** — Discover new categories of magical boons through wizard contacts. Early runs have a small boon pool; later runs have enormous variety.
- **Wizard Contacts** — Meet new wizard NPCs who appear in future runs as shop vendors, blessing givers, or rescue targets. Each has a personality and a boon specialty.
- **Family Affinities** — Unlock new magical affinities per character (e.g., Andrew gains access to earth-magic boons, John gains access to lightning-tech boons). These expand each character's build space without making them stronger by default.
- **Route Possibilities** — Unlock alternate room types, secret paths, and new encounter variants that get shuffled into the procedural pool.
- **Event Types** — New family moments, wizard encounters, and story scenes become available.
- **Challenge Modifiers** — After first successful escape, unlock difficulty modifiers (analogous to Hades' Pact of Punishment) that increase challenge and rewards.
- **Story Revelations** — Key narrative beats unlock between runs, advancing the overarching story of the Bell family, MaRC, and the wizard community. Multiple successful escapes are required to reach the true ending.

**Progression Pace:** A player should feel meaningful new options appearing every 2–3 runs. The first successful escape should happen around runs 8–15 depending on skill. The true ending should require ~30–40 runs of varied engagement.

## 8. Magic System

### Thematic Anchor

Magic is inherited, latent, and awakened through emotional intensity. Bea's accidental fireball and her subsequent reset power are the catalyst that starts awakening dormant magic in the entire Bell family. Each reset loop amplifies this effect. The wizard community — hidden in plain sight throughout the town — recognizes what's happening and begins helping.

### Per-Character Magic Identity

| Character | Magic Source | Expression | Arc |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Andrew** | Channeled through Bea | Ranged magic via shoulder-cast; protective wards | From "borrowing" Bea's magic to discovering his own latent shielding/earth magic |
| **Heather** | Amplification | Enhances others' magic; counter-pulses; resonance fields | From unconscious empathy to deliberate magical amplification and healing |
| **John** | Unconscious infusion | Magic leaks into his gadgets, making them supernatural | From "lucky coincidence" to intentional enchantment of his inventions |
| **Luna** | Instinctual | Speed, sensing danger, disruption howls | Animals in this world are naturally more attuned to magic; Luna was always a little magical |
| **Bea** | Raw power | Unstable, enormous, reality-bending potential | From dangerous accidents to focused, wondrous mastery (the meta-progression spine) |

### Boon Flavor Sources

Boons are themed around public-domain magical traditions and folklore archetypes. This gives the magic system a rich, collectible, "legendary" feeling without touching copyrighted material.

- **Elemental** — Fire, ice, lightning, earth, wind, water. Classic and immediately readable.
- **Celestial** — Star magic, moonlight, solar flares. Powerful, rare, dramatic.
- **Nature** — Growth, thorns, beast spirits, woodland wards. Tied to the Outer Wild area.
- **Artifice** — Rune-craft, enchantment, animated objects. Ties to John's gadget identity.
- **Warding** — Shields, barriers, reflections, absorption. Ties to Andrew's protector identity.
- **Resonance** — Harmony, amplification, sympathy, emotional magic. Ties to Heather's catalyst identity.
- **Primal** — Instinct, speed, ferocity, sensing, howl magic. Ties to Luna's animal magic.
- **Chaos** — Wild magic, unpredictable effects, reality glitches. Ties to Bea's unstable power.

## 9. Enemy Design

### MaRC Enemy Roster

**Tier 1 — Grunts (faceless, uniformed, impersonal)**

| Enemy | Role | Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **MaRC Agent** | Basic melee | Approaches and swings a baton. Low health. Comes in groups. The punching bag that makes you feel powerful. |
| **MaRC Shield Agent** | Blocker | Holds a riot shield. Must be hit from behind, launched over, or guard-broken. Teaches positioning. |
| **Suppression Agent** | Ranged pressure | Fires net-launcher or stun rounds from distance. Forces players to close gaps or dodge. |
| **Tracker Drone** | Alert/debuff | Small flying unit that spots the family and calls in reinforcements. Priority target — destroy before it completes its scan. |

**Tier 2 — Specialists (equipped, more tactical)**

| Enemy | Role | Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **Gadget Specialist** | Area denial | Deploys floor traps, flash grenades, and smoke screens. Creates zones the player must navigate around. |
| **Anti-Magic Technician** | Debuff/counter | Carries a field generator that suppresses boon effects in a radius. Must be destroyed or the technician must be defeated to restore magic. |
| **Capture Unit** | Grapple threat | Attempts to restrain a family member with energy cuffs. If successful, the grabbed character is disabled until an ally frees them. Creates urgency. |
| **Riot Mech** | Heavy/tank | Armored suit with slow powerful attacks. High health, but telegraphed and punishable. Area-denial stomp attacks. |

**Tier 3 — Elites and Bosses (visible faces, personality)**

| Enemy | Role | Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **Pursuit Officer** | Fast hunter | Chases the highest-threat family member relentlessly. Fast, aggressive, dodge-capable. Mini-boss quality in regular encounters. |
| **Field Commander** | Director | Doesn't fight directly at first — stands back, buffs allies, calls formations. Eliminating the Commander weakens the squad. When alone, fights desperately. |
| **MaRC Boss Agents** | Boss-tier | Unique characters with names, dialogue, multi-phase fights, and distinct personalities. Range from bumbling-but-dangerous to genuinely threatening. |

### Scaling Philosophy

Enemy difficulty scales through **composition complexity**, not stat inflation:

- **Early run:** Small groups of grunts. Maybe 1 shield agent.
- **Mid run:** Mixed squads: grunts + shield + suppression. Gadget specialists appear. First anti-magic tech.
- **Late run:** Full tactical squads: shield wall + suppression cover + anti-magic field + capture units flanking. Elites leading squads.
- **Boss encounters:** Unique enemy with multiple phases, squad support, and environmental mechanics.

### Elite Modifiers

Any standard enemy can appear with an elite modifier that makes them a priority threat:

- **Shielded** — Energy barrier absorbs the first few hits.
- **Armored** — Reduced damage from light attacks; must use heavies or magic.
- **Swift** — Moves and attacks faster. Dodges more.
- **Rallying** — Buffs nearby allies with damage or speed.
- **Regenerating** — Slowly recovers health if not hit for a few seconds.
- **Volatile** — Explodes on defeat, dealing area damage. Must be finished at range.

## 10. Procedural Design

### Philosophy

Procedural generation serves authored quality, never replaces it. Every room the player enters should feel intentionally designed, even though the *sequence* of rooms is randomized.

### Room Design

Each biome has a library of **handcrafted room chunks** (target: 15–25 per biome at full production; 5–8 for prototype).

A room chunk defines:
- Physical layout (platforms, walls, obstacles, interactive objects)
- Enemy spawn points and wave logic
- Environmental hazards or interactive elements
- Entry and exit positions
- Lighting and mood

Rooms are tagged with metadata:
- Biome
- Difficulty tier (easy / medium / hard)
- Encounter type (combat / elite / event / shop / boss)
- Pacing tag (opener / breather / escalation / climax)

### Sequencing Rules

The procedural sequencer assembles rooms following pacing constraints:

1. Each biome starts with an easier room to establish the new environment.
2. No more than 2 hard combat rooms in a row without a breather (shop, event, or blessing).
3. Elite encounters appear in the middle third of a biome, never first or last.
4. The last room before a miniboss is always a shop or blessing (the "calm before the storm").
5. Reward-preview doors respect pacing: if the player just fought hard, at least one door should offer a non-combat option.

### Route Branching

At each door choice, the player exercises agency over their run pacing:
- Choose the combat door for a boon reward and XP.
- Choose the shop door to spend resources and shore up weaknesses.
- Choose the event door for a story moment and a surprise reward.
- Choose the elite door for a harder fight with a rarer boon.

The system ensures that regardless of chosen path, the run hits all required pacing beats (rising difficulty, adequate boon density, mandatory miniboss gates).

## 11. Prototype Scope

### Goal

Build the smallest thing that proves the fun. The prototype answers five questions:

1. **Is the combat fun?**
2. **Is the family fantasy working?**
3. **Is the run structure exciting?**
4. **Does procedural content still feel crafted?**
5. **Do players want another run immediately?**

### Prototype Boundaries

| Include | Exclude |
|---|---|
| 1 biome (Neighborhood) | Full 4-biome content |
| 2 playable characters (Andrew + Heather) | John and Luna (stub or cut for prototype) |

| Keyboard + gamepad input | Touch controls |

| 1 meta-unlock (new boon added to pool after first run) | Full meta-progression |
| Run timer: ~8–12 minutes (1 biome slice) | Full 25–35 minute runs |

### Recommended Tech Stack

**Phaser 3 with TypeScript**

Rationale for a solo developer maximizing quality with minimum tooling friction:
- Mature, battle-tested 2D game framework with the largest community in web game dev.
- Built-in systems for everything a beat 'em up needs: sprite animation, physics, input management (keyboard + gamepad), camera control, tilemaps, particle effects, audio.
- TypeScript support provides autocomplete, type safety, and faster iteration with fewer bugs.
- Extensive documentation, examples, and community plugins reduce time spent solving framework problems.
- Excellent performance on modern browsers.
- The "2D gameplay with 3D-presented visuals" goal is achieved through art direction (painterly sprites, layered parallax, lighting effects), not engine-level 3D — Phaser handles this perfectly.
- As an AI copilot, I can write and iterate Phaser + TypeScript code very effectively, maximizing the value of agent-assisted development.

**Project Setup:**
- Phaser 3 + TypeScript + Vite (fast dev server, instant HMR)
- ECS-light architecture (scenes, game objects, component-like systems) rather than a heavy ECS framework
- Simple state machine for game/run/combat state management

## 12. Milestone Plan

### M1 — Combat Feel (Target: ~2 weeks)

**Deliverables:**
- 1 playable character (Andrew) in a test arena
- Basic light chain (3 hits + finisher), heavy attack, block
- 1 enemy type (MaRC Agent) with approach, attack, hitstun, and defeat behavior
- Hit-feel pass: hitstop, screenshake, knockback, placeholder SFX
- Belt-scrolling movement with dynamic camera (follow player, subtle zoom on hits)
- Keyboard + gamepad input

**Done When:** Hitting things feels great. A 30-second loop of punching MaRC agents is satisfying enough to repeat.

### M2 — Family + Run Structure (Target: ~3 weeks)

**Deliverables:**
- 2nd character (Heather) with distinct combo chain and parry mechanic
- Character select screen
- 3 enemy types (Agent, Shield Agent, Suppression Agent)
- 5–8 room chunks for Neighborhood biome
- Room-to-room flow with 2-door reward preview selection
- 1 miniboss (Neighborhood Squad Leader)
- Boon pickup system: 5 boons, 2 rarity tiers, stacking
- 1 wizard blessing room (choose 1 of 3 boons)
- 1 shop room (spend run currency on boons or healing)
- Basic run loop: start → rooms → miniboss → win/lose → restart
- Co-op architecture: 2-player local co-op functional

**Done When:** A run through the Neighborhood biome feels like an escape — tense, escalating, with meaningful choices and a satisfying miniboss climax. Two different runs feel different because of boon choices and room order.

### M3 — Prototype Complete (Target: ~2 weeks)

**Deliverables:**
- Polish pass on all combat (timing, balance, feel)
- 1 meta-unlock: completing a run adds a new boon to the pool for subsequent runs
- Hub screen between runs (minimal: character select + "start run" + unlock notification)
- Heather's catalyst mechanic: proximity buff to Andrew in co-op
- Co-op synergy: combo chain bonus when both players hit the same enemy
- Revive mechanic (timed proximity revive)
- 1 premium visual target mockup: one room fully art-directed to show the visual ambition (painterly style, lighting, parallax, effects)
- Basic sound design pass: hits, magic, UI, ambient
- Title screen, pause menu, run-end screen

**Done When:** Someone who has never seen the game can pick it up, play a run, and want to immediately play another one. The premium mockup screen makes them excited about what the full game will look like.

## 13. Risks & Watchouts

- **Combat feel is everything.** If M1 combat doesn't feel great, nothing else matters. Do not advance to M2 until hitting enemies is satisfying. Budget extra time here if needed.
- **Scope creep on characters.** 4 characters is the vision, but the prototype only needs 2. Do not start John or Luna until Andrew and Heather are fun. Each character is essentially a new game's worth of moveset design.
- **Procedural vs. authored tension.** Random rooms can easily feel soulless. Invest in room chunk quality and sequencing rules. A run with 6 good rooms beats a run with 12 mediocre ones.
- **Boon balance is a tar pit.** In the prototype, don't try to balance boons perfectly. Make them all feel powerful. Balancing is a production-phase concern.
- **Visual ambition vs. prototype speed.** Use placeholder art freely. The single "visual target" mockup room exists to prove the art direction; everything else can be rough.
- **Solo developer energy management.** The milestone plan is sequential and focused. Resist the urge to build systems in parallel. Finish M1 before starting M2.
- **Co-op readability.** Belt-scrolling with 2+ characters and multiple enemies gets visually busy fast. Prioritize distinct character silhouettes and clear enemy telegraphs from day one.

## 15. Success Criteria

The prototype is a success if:

1. **A player finishes a run and immediately starts another.** The combat is fun enough and the structure is varied enough to demand "one more try."
2. **Players can articulate the family fantasy.** They say things like "I love how Andrew carries Bea" or "Heather's parry is so satisfying" — not just "the combat is fine."
3. **Two different runs feel meaningfully different.** Different boon combinations and room sequences create distinct experiences.
4. **The visual target mockup makes people excited.** Even surrounded by placeholder art, the one polished room proves the game can look premium.
5. **Co-op is more fun than solo.** Two players together should feel like they're *actually* escaping as a family, not just two characters on the same screen.
